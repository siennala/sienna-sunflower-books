Blue Jay

By Sienna Sunflower

Chapter One
The First Day

If there's one thing that scares me the most, it would be dying alone. Not the dying part itself, but rather the part where I take my last breath and there's no one there except for me. I know that nothing should scare me anymore, but I can't seem to shake the fear. It's latched itself onto me too tightly-dug its poisoned claws into my mind too deeply.

I lay on the hard floor of my studio apartment. Even though the sun had long risen, the apartment is still dark. My apartment only has one window, and just last year, another high rise rose up from the ground to obscure the sky. What was once a window to the rare sun is now nothing more than drab concrete walls.

My head throbs, and I slowly sit up. I blow my dirty blonde hair out of my eyes as I wince at my headache. Today is the first day of school: the first day of eleventh grade.

I rise to my feet and stumble over to the corner of my room where I've left the backpack I mercifully discarded throughout the summer. It doesn't take me long to fill it with my binders-the same ones I used last year and the year before. Finally, I reach into the pockets of my oversized sweatpants for my glasses and slide them onto my face. The glasses help a lot.

After slinging my backpack over my shoulders, I make my way to the door of my unit. Before I exit, I turn around one more time and survey the empty space. I have almost no belongings. All of my clothes had been given to me by my neighbors after they deemed that the clothing I had was either too worn down or stretched out of shape to use anymore. I wasn't complaining. I don't care what I wear, but everyone else seems to.

I exit my apartment. The long hallway beyond is quiet. It's still early enough in the morning that everyone is either still asleep or has already left for work. I close the door to my apartment behind me and silently pad off down the hallway. My footsteps are the only sound as they echo on the concrete stairs, and my breath puffs out in front of me in the chilly morning air.

Even though it's still technically summer, the mornings are still unapologetically cold. But it won't take long for the temperatures to rise to an almost stifling heat. I can almost smell the unpleasant scent of too many bodies packed into tight hallways, almost hear the roar of voices, almost feel the suffocation.

When I reach the ground floor of the building, I push open the front door and step outside. And almost immediately, the sound of cars and people moving about hits me full in the face. I nearly choke on the heavy cloud of pollution in the air. The sky is as gray as the concrete buildings shooting out of the ground to scrape it. All the new buildings are at least fifty stories tall from what I remembered the last time I attempted to count the dizzying number of windows. The pollution is especially bad today, and I hastily pull my sweatshirt up over my nose and mouth.

From the ground, I can barely see the sky as I walk to the bus stop. The closer I get to downtown, the more and more the sky is blocked from view. Everything is painted in shades of dull gray. It hasn't rained in months now and the sparse plants speckling the area are a dead, dry brown. There isn't a single bird in the sky. The city is a monotonous void completely deprived of life and sunlight.

When I finally arrive at the bus stop, the poor air quality has begun to irritate my eyes. I squint through the haze that has settled over the ground. Surprisingly enough, I'm the first person here. I don't have to wait long before other teens around my age slowly amble up to the bus stop. All of them are wearing backpacks and have earbuds jammed into their ears. Some of them are in masks, others seemingly immune to the particles that are heavy in the air. They ignore me, and I ignore them.

If I listen carefully, I can hear the faint sounds of the music playing around me. All the different songs mingle and merge together into a hum of incoherent noise. Sometimes I wish I could stare mindlessly at a phone screen as well, but I can't afford such things.

Before long, headlights glow through the haze as the bus emerges. There's a soft hissing sound as the doors open and we all file on board. I fumble in my backpack for my bus card and quickly scan it before making my way toward the free seats at the back.

I take a seat next to the window, and since it's still early enough in the morning, the seat next to mine remains vacant for the duration of the ride. Forty-five minutes later, the bus comes to a stop in front of the main entrance to the school and we all file off. The sound of loud chatter hits me in the face the moment the doors to the bus swing open.

My head starts to throb the moment my feet touch the pavement. Loud. Everything is so loud. One step at a time, I have to remind myself as I look at all of the people standing in clumps around the front of the school.

I creep away from the bus and slowly make my way toward the entrance. My lungs already feel as if they're constricting more and more the closer I get to the entrance. No one bats an eye in my direction as I climb the steps to the main building with my sweatshirt still pulled over my nose and mouth, and my hands clenched tightly on the straps of my backpack. My backpack itself looks battered enough that I doubt anyone will try to rip it off me, but I never know what the pickpockets are up to.

The sun has risen higher in the sky, and the gray has changed from a dark shade of dismal misery to a slightly lighter one. I make a beeline through the courtyard and head in the direction of the administration section of the school. Since I can't have my schedule sent out to me digitally, I need to pick up a hard copy in person.

I keep my head down as I pass group after group of students lingering in the hallways. I've never been good with people my age. Something about talking to them makes my head spin, my voice pitch up, and my breathing difficult. I think it has something to do with the fact that I want to fit in with them, and trying to figure out how to be perceived is a daunting task. Thankfully, people don't normally talk to me, and I don't talk to them.

I stop at the door to my counselor's office and knock a few times with a shaky hand before entering. "Hello." My voice comes out in a strained sort of way. I can't remember the last time I used it.

"Oh, hello dear." The woman sitting behind the desk swivels in her chair to face me before continuing. "Are you here to pick up a schedule?"

I nod as I pull my sweatshirt down from my face.

"Name?"

"Julia. Julia Wright."

"Alright, Julia. Just give me a moment and I'll have it printed out for you."

"Thank you." I take a step backward to watch as the counselor searches for my name on her computer. Her long nails tap over the keys. As she prints out the sheet of paper, I glance around the small office. The posters covering the walls bring color to the otherwise dull room.

"Here, Julia." The counselor reaches over her desk to hand me the piece of paper. "If anything needs to be rearranged, don't hesitate to find me. I'm in my office before, during, and after school."

"Thank you," I say again as I take the paper from her. It's still warm from the printer, and I exit her office before glancing down at it. I can't seem to remember which classes I chose during the previous school year for junior year, but I've ended up with a pretty standard-looking spread. I don't recognize any of the classroom numbers or teacher names. Still staring at my schedule, I start making my way to my first-period classroom. English is first.

It's lunch in no time. The bell rings and the hallways explode in a roar of chatter. I let the wave of students carry me out into the courtyard. Just as I predicted, the temperature has risen drastically and I have to turn back into the school building to avoid baking.

I take a seat on one of the staircases and set my backpack on my legs as I stare into space. In all of the classes I've had so far, I've only recognized a handful of students I shared classes with last year. New people are fine. Old people are also fine. They're all the same.

I reach into my backpack and pull out my schedule. I still have American History, Math, and Intro to Astrophysics left until the day is officially over. Intro to astrophysics. My eyes linger on the last class. I don't remember picking this one. Fujiwara, the name of the teacher reads.

I stare at my schedule for a long time. I can see other students passing me on the staircase out of the corner of my eye. I can feel their gaze on me as they look for a brief moment before moving on.

I finally fold up my schedule and place it back inside my backpack. It would be nice to eat lunch with someone, but friends are just too difficult to make and even more difficult to keep. Last year in tenth grade, the group of girls who had decided I could follow them around ignored me for the most part. When they did talk to me, it was always to point out how I was the only one of them without a phone or social media.

I had stuck with them for a while, but when I realized I was being excluded from all of their after-school hangouts and gatherings, I gave up. It had only lasted for a month before I was on my own again. I just couldn't keep up. I never understood any of their conversations about their online posts or the gossip they seemed to like so much. All of it just made my head hurt. With hanging around such a large group of people, there were always too many eyes and too many whispers.

In a blink of an eye, the last class of the day is upon me. I walk into the astrophysics classroom and marvel at the posters of planets and stars that decorate the walls. A galaxy-it's a pretty little galaxy of night and stars and space. Windows line the wall on the far side of the classroom and give a clear view of the polluted sky and haze-shrouded high rises outside.

"Welcome to Intro to Astrophysics," a voice says from the front of the classroom. I turn to find a woman in her late thirties standing behind the desk. "The seating chart has been projected on the whiteboard. These will be your seats for the entirety of the first semester. If you find that you have any problems with these arrangements, feel free to tell me and I can adjust you accordingly."

I nod along with the other students who are still filing into the classroom. I quickly spot the picture of myself on the board and shuffle over to the desk I'll be sharing with one of my other classmates. It's a picture from last year-and a very awful one at that.

I set my backpack under my desk by my feet and pull out my deteriorating binder. As more students enter the classroom, our teacher repeats her instructions. It doesn't take long for someone to take the chair next to mine. I turn my head slightly, looking at my new deskmate out of the corner of my eye. I don't think I've ever seen this boy before. He's tall and pale with short brown hair.

"What's your name?" the boy says in a way of greeting. His voice is loud. A little too loud, and I flinch ever so slightly at his volume.

"J-Julia," I manage to get out.

"You're joking. Your name can't be Julia."

I blink. "I'm sorry?"

"My girlfriend's name is Julia," the boy says as if it's obvious.

"Julia Bennet?" I nearly let out a laugh. Of course it's Julia Bennet. She's the person I'm always confused with. Whenever people hear the name Julia, they immediately think of Julia Bennet. They always think of Julia Bennet as the only Julia, which is silly because Julia is quite a common name. Most of the people in my grade know that there are two Julias: the popular Julia, and then the other Julia.

The boy looks me up and down. "So you're the other Julia."

I just nod. I've already reached my speaking limit for the day. Are you going to tell me your name? I want to ask. But then on second thought, I find that I don't care enough to ask the question.

The boy just turns away from me, losing all interest completely. Good, I let out a breath. Things are better if we don't have to talk. The bell rings again, signaling the start of class, and we all turn to the dark-haired woman at the front of the classroom expectantly.

"Good afternoon everyone," our teacher says with a warm smile. "I'm Ms. Fujiwara. I know this is the last class of the day, and I hope you can bear with me."

It takes me the entirety of the class period to figure out what my deskmate's name is: Leon. He mostly ignores me, and it wasn't until one of my other classmates' addressed him by his name that I finally realized what it was. It's only been no more than ten minutes, but the impression I'm getting from Leon is not a good one. I just hope we don't end up having to do any group work.

After we make a trip down to the textbook room to collect our books for the year, the class returns to our classroom and I take a seat at my desk. The cover of my astrophysics textbook is a night sky full of stars. There's something beautiful about it . . . I've never seen the stars before. I know they're there somewhere behind the thick haze of pollution and city lights, but I still have yet to see them with my own eyes.

They say that the sky is like an ocean. An ocean of blue. Sometimes it's clear, other times it's brushed with clouds. Is it like an ocean? I can't say. Even though I've seen pictures of the ocean before, I've never seen it in person either. Since we are so inland, there aren't any oceans or seas around. The largest body of water around is the murky-looking lake toward the east side of the city. And that lake barely even passes as a lake.

As I stare at the cover of my textbook, a hand suddenly reaches over with a permanent marker in its fingers. Just before the marker can touch the cover of my textbook, I quickly snatch my book away and turn to find Leon looking at me in a nonplussed sort of way.

"I'm bored," Leon says as he flips through his own textbook. "You won't let me scribble on the cover of your book?"

I vigorously shake my head. That's a violation of school property, I want to say. It's not like our school has much funding in the first place. If you ruin a perfectly good textbook, where are they going to find the funds to replace it? You should respect other people's property especially if you're not going to pay for the damage yourself. But none of that comes out, and I'm left sitting in silence.

"Fine." Leon loses all interest in me for a second time today. "You're nothing like Julia Bennet at all."

Did you really think I would be? I just shake my head again, inwardly cringing as Leon's marker squeaks over the cover of his textbook as he slowly scribbles out the stars.

Finally, the last bell rings and there's a shuffling as everyone stands up from their chairs to swing their backpacks over their shoulders. Leon's one of the first people out the door, and without him around I feel like I can finally breathe properly again.

I tug my sweatshirt back over my mouth and nose as I shuffle out of the classroom. The pollution has worsened again by the time I arrive at the bus stop. Normally, I go to the school's library to do my homework, but since today is the first day, the library isn't open to students.

Fortunately, I don't have any real homework today. All I need to do is get my syllabuses signed by a parent or guardian.

The sky is still gray when I reach the doors to my apartment building. The only thing that has changed since this morning is the heat, and I grimace to myself as I pull open the front doors to the building and step into the lobby. I slowly make my way up the concrete stairs. My footsteps echo in the stillness, and the sound of cars is muffled from somewhere far below.

It feels like I'm walking forever as I pass my usual exit on the eleventh floor and continue higher and higher all the way to the twenty-fifth floor. I walk down the hallway, and when I finally come upon the last door, I gingerly extend a finger and tap the doorbell.

I don't have to wait long before the door swings open to reveal a white-haired woman in her sixties. "Julia, dear." She looks down at me, and I pull my sweatshirt down from my nose and mouth. "Do you need something?"

"Do you think you could sign some things for school for me, Ms. Akram?"

"Of course." Ms. Akram beckons me to follow her into her apartment. When it comes to school-related things, Ms. Akram helps me fill out forms in the name of my guardian. She also happens to be the manager and landlord of this building. She goes out a lot and always asks me to come to her apartment to take care of her cats while she's away. I don't mind looking after Ms. Akram's cats. All of them are very sweet, and Ms. Akram also lets me use her TV.

The moment the door to the apartment swings shut behind me, there's a soft meow and the jigging of bells as something furry leaps up onto my backpack.

"I'm sorry, Julia dear," Ms. Akram says apologetically as she pulls the cat off me and scoops it into her arms. "You haven't been over at all during the summer and the cats miss you."

I give Ms. Akram a small smile. "I think the cats enjoy your company."

"I'm almost fully recovered," Ms. Akram says as I hand her the syllabuses for my school. "See? I don't need the crutches anymore either. I'll be out and about in no time." Just before summer started, Ms. Akram had sprained one of her legs while walking up the stairs, and she had to spend the entirety of the summer in her apartment because of it. "Did you hear the news?" Ms. Akram asks as she hands back my papers. "The city voted on lowering the factory regulations again! As if the air quality isn't already bad enough!"

"They did?" My heart sinks.

"It's those good-for-nothing corporations backing those good-for-nothing politicians," Ms. Akram says. "Would you like to stay for dinner, Julia dear?"

"What? I couldn't, Ms. Akram! I don't want to trouble you."

"You're no trouble." Ms. Akram gives me a warm smile as she shuffles over to the kitchen with a noticeable limp. "If you don't want to keep an old lady like myself company, I understand."

"I'll stay," I say quickly. I won't turn down the opportunity to stay out of my own dreary apartment for as long as possible. Ms. Akram is kind, and as long as she isn't bothered by me, I'm happy to stay.

"Wonderful, dear," Ms. Akram calls from the kitchen. "I haven't cooked for anyone in a while. Oh, this is going to be fun! Feel free to turn on the TV."

I nod even though I know Ms. Akram can't see me. I set my backpack down by the door and make my way over to the couch. The moment I take a seat, I can barely grab the TV remote before one of Ms. Akram's cats has jumped onto my lap.

I think Ms. Akram is lonely. Even with all of her cats to keep her company, there's something about Ms. Akram that gives me the impression that she's gone through her fair share of difficulties. Ms. Akram has a framed photo next to her TV of a much younger-looking version of herself accompanied by a man and three children. I've never asked her why she lives alone in this apartment or what happened to her family, but I've never had the heart to bring it up.

When I hit the power button, the news is the first thing that pops up on the screen. There's been another mass shooting. Another collapsed building. Another fire. Disaster after disaster.

I quickly change the channel and pull up the show I had started watching the last time I was over at Ms. Akram's apartment.

I absently stare at the screen as I stroke the soft fur of the cats sitting around me. Eventually I lift my gaze to the window and the concrete walls beyond. Buildings are everywhere. Sometimes Ms. Akram would tell me of a time where you could still see a horizon that wasn't blocked by skyscrapers; a time where you could still see the sky and where birds danced among the clouds. The overall life expectancy has already dropped drastically, and yet the people in charge still won't bat an eye.

Ms. Akram reemerges from the kitchen. "Food's ready."

I turn off the TV and try my best to dislodge the cats on my lap before standing up. "Thank you." I hurry over to the kitchen to wash the cat hair from my hands.

Ms. Akram tells me about the news and the recent political developments as we eat. Even though Ms. Akram tries her best to be optimistic for my sake, we both know if things don't change soon, it will be too late. Humanity is its own downfall. This is our fate.

✦ ✦ ✦

After I leave Ms. Akram's apartment, I make my way up the stairs to the roof of the building. The gray concrete of the surrounding buildings shoot up around me. I raise my head to the gray sky. No sun, no moon. Just gray. No stars, no sky. This city is a concrete jungle and we are the animals that inhabit it.

I'll have to return to my own apartment soon. Staying outside too long will cause my eyes to start watering from the smog.

My head gives a painful throb, and I lower my gaze. My eyes fall on the edge of the roof. There's no railing and the concrete seems to give away into nothingness.

My head throbs again. I turn back to the stairs. The pollution is already starting to get to me.

Back in my dark apartment, I lay on the floor with my textbooks scattered around me. I hold up my astrophysics textbook and gaze up at the star-flecked sky on the cover. Maybe this is what it's like to look up at the night sky.

Chapter Two
A Memory of Dawn

"Look what I drew!" I proudly hold up my paper in one hand and an assortment of crayons in the other.

"Wow!" Teddy's eyes sparkle as he reaches across the table to tentatively take the paper from me.

I smile before hopping up from my seat and make my way around to the other side of the table to look over Teddy's shoulder at his own drawing. "So many stars!"

"The night sky is full of stars," Teddy explains proudly as he points to his drawing.

"Should I add stars to my drawing as well?"

"No." Teddy shakes his head knowingly as he lays my drawing next to his. "This is the daytime sky. You can see the clouds in the day while you can see the stars at night."

"Oh, that's right."

"I'm almost done." Teddy takes one of the crayons I offer him and skillfully adds the finishing touches to his drawing. "Once I finish it, we can draw the time between day and night together!"

"Okay!" I sit down next to Teddy. Even though I can hear the other children laughing and talking among themselves from the other tables, I like to pretend that it's only Teddy and me. Teddy is my best friend in the entire world.

"I'm done!" Teddy proudly sets down his crayons. I clap my hands as I marvel at the different shades of midnight. "Wouldn't it be nice to see such a sky in real life?" Teddy stares wistfully at our drawings.

I nod as I reach out a hand and touch the page of night with a finger.

"Do you want to draw the sunset now?" Teddy asks brightly. "We still have around ten minutes or so until free time is over. What colors do we use for the sunset?"

"Red, orange, yellow, blue, pink, purple." I count off on my fingers.

"Alright!" Teddy jumps to his feet before taking me by the hand. Together, we hurry over to the big crayon box at the back of the classroom. After rummaging through the box, we return to our table and get to work. It's very fun scribbling over the paper with the colors, changing the white backdrop to a sky composed of hundreds of zigzagging lines.

When we finish, the two of us place our new drawing between the pictures of day and night.

"Perfect!" I proudly admire our work. Teddy and I give each other a high five before we get to work returning all of our crayons to the box.

"I'll bring the drawings back to our room for us later," Teddy tells me.

Just as I nod my head, the door to the classroom swings open and our teacher walks in. "It looks like class is starting."

Everyone scrambles to take their seats at their desks. "Good afternoon, third graders," our teacher says. "It's time to continue our lessons."

✦ ✦ ✦

Teddy and I sit next to each other in our room. Both of us are silent as we continue to wait. It's Teddy's turn to be called on today, and I give his hand a squeeze.

"Should I be nervous?" Teddy asks. I can hear the slight tremor in his voice.

"It's not bad." I shake my head. "It's really not that bad. You'll be back before you know it, and then we can continue to play."

Teddy nods.

Suddenly there's a knock on the door. There's a soft clicking noise as it swings open to reveal a middle-aged man standing in the doorway. This man is Mr. Wright, and he's in charge. Or so everyone says.

"Tamish," Mr. Wright calls.

I give Teddy's hand a final squeeze before helping him rise to his feet. At the apprehensive expression on my friend's face, I turn to Mr. Wright. "Can I go with him?"

"No, Julia." Mr. Wright looks at me. "We already called on you two days ago. It's Tamish's turn today."

"Can I walk with him at least?"

"No."

I give Teddy an apologetic look as I start to slip my hand out of his. Teddy clings to my hand, his face paling.

"The sooner you get it over with," I say softly, "the sooner you can come back."

Teddy swallows as he slowly lets go of my hand. I offer Teddy a smile as he steps out of our room. He looks back at me one more time before following Mr. Wright down the hall. The door swings shut with a click as it relocks itself.

I slowly walk over to the back wall of our small room; the wall where we hang up all of our drawings. Our three drawings of the sky are a nice new addition. The colors add life to the otherwise plain room.

After staring at the drawings for a while, I walk over to my bed and flop down onto it. I turn my head and stare absently at Teddy's empty bed opposite mine. Even though the room is small and windowless, Teddy and I spend most of our time here. It doesn't bother us that there isn't much space. It's still more than enough room to play with our stuffed animals and make up story after story about their adventures.

Just as a small smile flickers acrosses my face, my head gives a painful throb. I still as my head throbs again. Clenching my fists, I pull my pillow over my head. It's not that bad, I tell myself. Maybe I just worked too hard in class today. Maybe my head is punishing me for even considering adding stars in the daytime sky.

I don't know when exactly I fell asleep, but I awake to Teddy shaking my shoulders. "Jay," he calls my name as I blink my eyes open. My headache has vanished.

"Teddy!" I sit bolt upright. "How are you?"

Teddy grins. "I'm fine. You were right-it wasn't all that bad."

"Good." I let out a breath.

Teddy takes a seat on the bed next to me, picking up the stuffed bear next to my pillow. "We still have a few hours before dinner. Want to play a game?"

I nod as I pick up a second stuffed bear. "Let's continue the adventures of the teddy bears!"

✦ ✦ ✦

Before long, it's time for bed. All the main lights have been turned off until it's time to wake up in the morning.

Teddy and I remove the blankets and pillows from our beds and lay them out on the floor between them. The two of us lay on our makeshift bed on the floor and stare up at the ceiling.

"Jay," Teddy says after a while. His voice is nothing more than a whisper.

"What is it?" I ask as I roll onto my side to face him.

"Do you know where that lamp is? I can't see anything in the dark."

"I'll go get the lamp." I stand up and hold my arms out in front of me as I feel my way around in the dark. I quickly find the lamp on the shelf, and with the press of a button, the plastic shade is filled with light. Our room is illuminated in a warm golden glow as I bring the lamp over to the floor and pass it to Teddy before laying back down.

Teddy smiles as the two of us crawl under the covers. We lay on our sides with the lamp between us, and the golden light gives Teddy's brown skin the illusion that it's glowing. This is our forest fort and we're the wooden supports. The residents of this fort are our stuffed bears, and they huddle around the lamp in the middle.

"I wish I could be a teddy bear," Teddy says wistfully as the two of us stare at the two bears sitting in front of the light with glowing fur. "I bet it's a pretty nice life."

"You are a teddy, though," I point out.

Teddy just laughs.

✦ ✦ ✦

After Teddy and I exit our classroom, the two of us follow our other classmates in a single file line down the hall. As we all make our way toward the cafeteria, a figure with dirty-blonde hair strides down the hallway.

"Quick!" Teddy tugs at my sleeve, his eyes falling on the person at the same time as mine. "Hide behind me!"

Too late. The blonde-haired girl spots me almost instantly. She's smiling as she walks over to me.

"Julia." The girl looks down at me. She's only a few years older than I am, but she's at least a head taller than me. "Have you been avoiding me?"

Teddy and I both look up at her silently.

"I'm doing just fine, thank you for asking." My sister looks back and forth between Teddy and me. "According to Mr. Wright, I happen to be the favorite so far out of all of the other students."

"Congratulations," I say.

"That's all you have to say?"

"Jay and I aren't trying to compete with you," Teddy says tiredly. "We've told you this before."

My sister has a disbelieving expression on her face. "You're not?"

I shake my head. "We're really not trying to compete with you."

"Genesis." Mr. Wright appears from around the corner. "Come, we've got another test to run."

My sister spins around to face Mr. Wright.

"Good luck," I say softly to my sister.

Genesis just sneers at me before she turns on her heel and follows Mr. Wright down the hall.

When she's out of earshot, Teddy and I exchange a glance.

"I don't get it." Teddy shakes his head as we trail after our classmates toward the cafeteria. "We're not even in the same grade. Wouldn't she want to compete with someone on the same level as her?"

I frown. "Maybe she thinks I'm on the same level as her since I'm her sister." Then I shake my head as well. "No, she always thinks that she's better than me. I've never been on the same level as her."

Teddy offers me a smile. "She's gone now. Come on, let's go get some lunch."

I nod as I let Teddy take me by the hand and lead me down the hall. Even though Genesis is my sister, or at least she's supposed to be, I can't seem to understand her at all. Teddy is a person I understand much better.

✦ ✦ ✦

Teddy and I are back in our room. The two of us are talking together on his bed when Teddy suddenly clutches his head.

"Teddy!" I turn to him in concern. "Are you okay?"

"My head." Teddy grimaces, and his fingers dig into his dark hair. "My head hurts all of a sudden."

"You should lay down." I quickly hop off of the bed to give Teddy more space. "Laying down helps if you start to feel dizzy."

Teddy nods as he takes my advice and lays down. "This happened to you as well?"

"It did." I take a seat on the floor next to Teddy's bed. "But it doesn't last very long. A few minutes at most."

Teddy hisses softly before looking over at me. "Jay, will you lay here with me?"

"Okay."

Teddy closes his eyes, taking a deep breath as he winces against his headache.

"It will be over soon." I wrap my arms around him, hugging him tightly.

"It will be over soon," Teddy echoes. But I can hear the pain in his voice, and it hurts me as well.

✦ ✦ ✦

"What are you drawing today?" Teddy peeks over my shoulder.

"I'm drawing the two of us." I show my friend my half-complete drawing. "This is you," I point to the bear-shaped blob on the left side of the page, "and this is me."

"Wow!" Teddy claps his hands together, impressed. "We really are teddy bears!"

There's a clicking sound as the door to the classroom swings open. "Good morning, fourth grade." Our teacher strides into the classroom. The first thing I notice is that we have a new teacher today. We usually go through about three or four teachers a year. I don't know what happened to them or where they went since I've never seen any of them again. Maybe they don't like teaching a bunch of elementary-aged children. Or maybe Mr. Wright's not paying them enough. He's the one in charge.

I jump to my feet, and my momentum sends me crashing into the wall. Teddy's voice is all around me. I can hear him, but I can't see him. Where is Teddy? I frantically search the room. Where is Teddy?

From somewhere far away, Genesis is laughing and clapping her hands together. I ignore her as I climb to my feet, using the wall beside me as a support. But I stagger again. The voices are deafening.

I'm hallucinating, I have to remind myself as my limbs fail me. I hit the floor in a daze. I'm sorry Teddy. I don't think I'll be able to avenge you after all.

I squeeze my eyes shut and curl into a ball as I clamp my hands over my ears. But no matter how hard I try, the voices still slip past my hands. They fill my head, and I can't tell if the screams that started are mine or just another hallucination.

I'm clawing at my ears as the roaring grows louder and louder. When I finally let go, I look on in horror as I hold them in front of me, the blood dripping off them is my own. I'm doing exactly what Genesis wants me to do. But what's the point in fighting it? I'm dying anyway.

Suddenly all of the noises stop. The room is deadly silent, and I blink. Genesis is still sitting across from me on the other side of the room. She's frowning slightly as she watches me. "Is that all, Julia?"

"What do you mean is that all?" I gasp out. My voice is raw and hoarse.

"That's strange." Genesis ignores me. "I thought that serum would cause more hallucinations than that. Whatever, though, I guess. Here." I blink as Genesis tosses a knife toward me. The blade skids across the smooth tiled floor. "My present to you. It shouldn't take long for the next wave of hallucinations to start."

I frown as I stare at the knife. Suddenly my head throbs again, and I stagger to my feet.

"Jay." Teddy's voice echoes, and suddenly he appears in front of me. "Jay, wait." Teddy's voice sounds strained as I make to clasp my hands over my ears again. I pause and lower them back to my sides as Teddy looks from me to Genesis and then back again. "If I told you I had a plan, would you be willing to use that knife on your sister?"

"I don't want to hurt her," I whisper.

Teddy shakes his head grimly. "Don't worry, I don't want you to kill her. You'll just be buying yourself some more time."

"What do you want me to do?"

Teddy draws closer before standing on his tiptoes to whisper in my ear. When he draws away, I stare at him with wide eyes.

"Are you ready?" Teddy asks softly.

I nod, and then I'm staggering as the voices roar through my head again. I stumble through the cacophony of sound. But even as I spin around and around and around, I know the path on which I'm headed-I'm heading toward where Genesis is watching me with a smile on her face.

As I near, Genesis doesn't seem to think anything of my approach. When I'm close enough, I suddenly whirl and swing the blade with all the speed I can muster.

Genesis barely has time to spring to her feet, and her hands close around my knife hand as she stares at me in shock. But that shock quickly melts away as she tosses me to the floor. My head and ears are ringing.

"Nice try." Genesis laughs as she crouches down to pry the blade out of my hand. "But you've got to be faster than that."

Before I can blink, Genesis is holding a gun to my forehead.

"That's right," Genesis purrs. "I've had my fun. Mr. Wright wants me to kill you anyway. We've both delayed long enough already, yes?"

"You won't really kill me," I hiss as I look up at my sister. "You still want to figure out how I survived that day. If you really want to know so desperately, I'll tell you. Teddy brought me back to life."

Genesis blinks. "That's it?"

"That's it."

"I don't believe it. He was practically dead himself when I checked on him. If he really had that much energy left, why not save himself?"

I let out a humorless laugh. "You really don't understand, do you? You haven't changed a bit, Genesis. It's not your fault that you don't understand, though. It's not your fault that you can't understand. It's Mr. Wright's fault. He's the one who did this to you-to us. Maybe in another life, we can actually be sisters. Maybe in another life things wouldn't have to end this way."

"Mr. Wright is brilliant." Genesis shakes her head. The muzzle of her gun is still glued to my forehead. "Mr. Wright made me strong. He made me who I am today."

"It's exactly because he made you who you are today that I pity you. You can't find joy in life. You can't find joy in the simple things that are all around us."

"Of course I can find joy." Genesis presses the gun harder, but I don't even flinch. "I find joy in being the best. I find joy in being the only one in the world with this sort of power!"

"But you aren't the best." I look at my sister in exasperation. "Even if you are now, there will always be new people rising up. Mr. Wright didn't do this solely for you, Genesis. At the end of the day, you're only his test subject. Please," I say quietly. "I'm dying. We both know it. Why won't you ever let me go?"

Genesis holds my gaze. "Once you're gone, there will be no one else left worthy of being my competitor."

I shake my head. "Once I'm gone, you won't have much time left either. You're dying as well, aren't you."

"I-" Genesis starts but then she claps a hand to her head, hissing in pain. "Go away!" Genesis suddenly yells. She swipes out at the air with fingers curled into claws. I yelp as Genesis shoves me away from her in her sudden frenzy, and there's a bang of a gunshot echoing through the room.

My heart is pounding in my chest as I look at the smoking bullet hole in the wall between the two of us.

"You're seeing me!" I blink as I stare at my sister in surprise. "Have I really been haunting you so much that you want to put me to death?"

"Who said I'm hallucinating you?" Genesis says a little too quickly.

"Why exactly am I haunting you so much?"

"Stay away!" Genesis points her gun around the room with crazed eyes.

"Genesis," I say firmly as I slowly climb to my feet.

Genesis freezes before whirling on me. Her gun is pointed straight at me. I raise my hands in the air in a gesture of surrender as I slowly take a step forward.

"What are you doing?" Genesis stares at me as I take another step.

I look up at my sister. "Maybe I was wrong. Maybe you do understand, after all."

Just like I did in that dream, I continue walking forward. Continue walking toward the girl with the dirty-blonde hair. When I reach her, I stare deep into her blue eyes before wrapping my arms around her and pulling her into an embrace.

"I want to believe that you understand," I breathe as I close my eyes. I can feel her arms moving, the muzzle of the gun resting on my back, and I squeeze my eyes shut, waiting for the pain. My heart is pounding away in my chest, and I know deep down that each beat might be its last.

And suddenly there's a bang, and I feel the bullet tear through me. I stagger. I wonder if Genesis can see the hurt in my eyes as I collapse to the floor. But then Genesis is falling too, and the gun clatters to the floor at her feet.

My eyes widened. The bullet Genesis had shot into me had flown straight through the two of us.

"We die together," Genesis coughs.

But before I can open my mouth, my vision turns red. Everything hurts as I lay there on the floor next to my sister.

If there's one thing that scares me the most, it would be dying alone. Not the dying part itself, but rather the part where I take my last breath and there's no one there except for me.

But as I stare at Genesis, I realize with a jolt that I'm not alone. I just never expected my sister to be the one lying beside me.

Suddenly there's the sound of footsteps running beyond the walls of the room, and then the door flies open as police charge in. I let out a laugh of relief. My breathing is shaky, and each breath is an effort within itself. The Fujiwaras had done their part, and I had done mine.

✦ ✦ ✦

"Julia," Miyoko is calling my name. I can hear the tears in her voice as I slowly crack an eye open to find myself lying in what appears to be a hospital room. I turn my head to find Miyoko sitting next to my hospital bed. Her inky hair is unbound and hangs limply over her shoulders.

"Miyoko." My voice is hoarse.

Miyoko's eyes widen as she realizes I'm awake. "Julia!"

"I'm awake." I smile softly as I meet her gaze. "What happened?"

"We found the underground lab and called the police," Miyoko quickly informs me. "When the police found you, you and your sister were both bleeding out on the floor of one of the rooms."

"How's Genesis? How's she doing now?"

"Genesis is dead," Miyoko says quietly.

"Dead?"

Miyoko's nod of confirmation is all I need before that empty feeling seems to spread through me. I should be glad. But instead, I just feel empty.

"Julia!" Mr. and Ms. Fujiwara rush into the hospital room. Their expressions are worried as they join their daughter at my bedside.

"How do you feel?" Ms. Fujiwara stares down at me.

I give them both a reassuring smile. "I'm fine."

"Fine?" Miyoko echoes quietly. Her eyes are sparkling with tears. "Liar. When were you going to tell us, Julia? When were you going to tell me?"

"What have I forgotten to tell you?"

"You didn't tell us that you're dying," Miyoko cries as she shakes her head. "The doctor was the one who revealed to us that you've been slowly dying for years now."

Ah yes. I let out a heavy sigh. "It's my fault for not telling you, Miyoko. In fact, it's my fault for not telling any of you. This I never lied to you about it, though-I don't think keeping it from you counts as lying. But there's a reason I didn't tell you: I didn't want any of you to worry about me any more than you already were. I was going to tell you eventually, but since you've found out before I've gotten the chance, I guess that means I've broken my promise of not keeping any more secrets. And for that, I'm sorry. I hope you can understand and can find it in your hearts not to blame me too much."

"I don't blame you at all." Miyoko sniffles as she shakes her head.

"You're too good, you know." I sigh as I turn to look up at the ceiling. My gaze snags on the windows lining the back wall and the cloudy gray sky beyond. "Did the doctor say how many days I have left?"

"Not many," Mr. Fujiwara says regretfully.

I sigh softly. That's alright. This is alright. I've lived much longer than even I expected myself to.

"Mr. Wright has been taken to jail." Ms. Fujiwara's tone darkens. "He'll never bother anyone ever again behind those bars. The evidence in that underground lab was overwhelming proof of what he did."

I close my eyes. "Good."

Miyoko is sobbing. "I don't want you to die, Julia. I want you to keep living. I want us to continue being friends."

I open my eyes again and smile softly. "I'll always be your friend, Miyoko. Even when I'm no longer around, it doesn't mean that I'll stop being your friend."

"Are you scared?" Miyoko asks quietly.

"Scared?" I repeat thoughtfully. "Yes. And no. Death is an unknown, and the unknown will always be frightening. But with all of you here by my side, I'm not afraid." I turn to look over at Mr. Fujiwara next. "Miyoko is such a wonderful girl-a wonderful friend. I hope you can continue to love and support her." Then I turn to Ms. Fujiwara. "I'm so glad you were able to be my teacher this past school year. Your class made school more bearable-you made school enjoyable even with all of the things that happened during the year." Finally, I turn to Miyoko. "Thank you. Thank you for everything."

"You sound like you're saying goodbye," Miyoko says fearfully. "Do you really have to go so soon?"

I can feel my heartbeat fading. "I don't think it's up to me anymore." But I'm not afraid. I'm not afraid.

"But after you're gone, who will be the city's superhero?"

"A superhero doesn't need special powers to be a superhero. A superhero is just someone who selflessly helps those in need. The city's next superhero could be anyone. In fact, the city's next superhero could be you."

"I will change the world," Miyoko says determinedly. "Just you wait."

"Good." I smile as I look at all three of the Fujiwaras. "I think the world needs some change."

Everything is slowing down. My thoughts, my breathing, my heartbeat. And even though I'm not sure what will come-even though I'm not sure what the world will be like after I'm gone, I'm sure it can change for the better. I have hope.

Epilogue

The sound of gentle footsteps makes its way through the grass as they pass tombstone after tombstone in the silent cemetery. It's early morning, and the blue sky has just emerged from the oranges and pinks of dawn.

After stopping to set down the pair of high heels she had been carrying, a beautiful young woman in an elegant office dress kneels down in front of a particular tombstone. She reaches up for a moment to tuck a strand of her inky hair behind an ear before pressing her hands together and closing her eyes. A wind picks up. It rustles the grass and sets the young woman's hair dancing.

She bows her head in silence. Then, the young woman finally rises and looks down at the tombstone for a long moment. She reaches into her purse and produces a bouquet of hand-picked flowers. Keeling down once again, she sets it at the head of the tombstone to replace the old bouquet that still lay there. The young woman watches the blue ribbon around the bouquet flutter in the wind for a long moment. After letting out a soft sigh, the young woman lifts her head. "It's another clear day today, Julia. I hope you're enjoying it too."
